Stone Education

What You’re Seeing in the Collection

These are likely stone identifications based on colour, texture, pattern, and shoreline wear. They are not lab-confirmed IDs, but they help explain the kinds of materials that appear throughout the collection.

Stone Tags Used in the Shop

These tags now appear across the collection to make the pendants feel more like a shoreline gallery and less like generic product listings.

Quartz / Milky Quartz

Tag: quartz • milky quartz • chalcedony

Quartz is common along Great Lakes shorelines and often appears white, cloudy, translucent, or lightly banded. It can look clean and luminous, especially when water-worn.

Seen in pieces like Pendant 10 and Pendant 23.

Quartzite

Tag: quartzite • pale quartzite

Quartzite forms when sandstone is compressed and recrystallized. It tends to be dense, smooth when shoreline-worn, and often appears cream, white, or lightly mineralized.

Seen in pieces like Pendant 2, Pendant 14, Pendant 18, and Pendant 29.

Jasper

Tag: jasper • brecciated jasper • orbicular jasper

Jasper is an opaque silica-rich stone that can appear in red, brown, green, cream, or spotted forms. It often has stronger patterning than quartzite and can look earthy or graphic depending on the mineral mix.

Seen in pieces like Pendant 8, Pendant 12, Pendant 13, Pendant 17, and Pendant 27.

Serpentine / Greenstone

Tag: serpentine • greenstone

Serpentine and related green shoreline stones often show soft green, grey-green, or pale mossy tones. Some are very smooth and quiet, while others include darker mineral inclusions.

Seen in pieces like Pendant 4, Pendant 16, Pendant 22, and Pendant 24.

Gneiss / Banded Metamorphic Stone

Tag: gneiss • banded stone

Gneiss and similar metamorphic stones often show natural layering or striping from mineral compression. These pieces can read like shoreline sediment lines, driftwood grain, or folded bands.

Seen in pieces like Pendant 3, Pendant 5, Pendant 19, and Pendant 21.

Granite / Diorite

Tag: granite • diorite • mineral-flecked stone

Granite and related igneous stones often show speckling, mixed mineral grain, or stronger contrast between light and dark sections. These stones tend to feel solid and geologically textured.

Seen in pieces like Pendant 11 and parts of Pendant 18.

Dendritic Stone

Tag: dendritic jasper • dendritic marble

Dendritic stones show branch-like dark mineral patterning that can look like roots, ink, or tiny landscape drawings. These patterns are naturally occurring mineral deposits, not cracks or stains.

Seen most clearly in Pendant 28.

Sea Glass

Tag: sea glass

Sea glass begins as discarded glass and becomes naturally frosted and softened through long exposure to water, sand, and stone. It has a different origin than the rock pendants, but still carries the shoreline story.

Seen in Pendant 1.

Collector’s Note

Tag: likely identification

Many shoreline finds sit somewhere between easy categories. Some pieces show qualities of multiple stone types, especially after years of shoreline tumbling and weathering.

That’s why the shop uses “Likely:” rather than claiming exact lab identification.

How to Read the Shop Tags

Colour

Green pieces may point toward serpentine, greenstone, or green jasper. White or translucent pieces often suggest quartz, chalcedony, or quartzite. Warm brown, peach, and red tones can indicate jasper, feldspar-rich material, or iron staining.

Pattern

Strong stripes or folds often suggest metamorphic stone like gneiss. Speckling can suggest granite or mineral-flecked igneous stone. Mossy, spotted, or branching marks often point toward inclusions or dendritic growth.

Surface

Smoother matte pieces are often heavily shoreline-worn. More textured pieces may still hold more of their original mineral grain or fractured structure.

Why This Matters

Adding stone tags helps each pendant feel documented and contextualized. It also gives buyers a better sense of what they’re looking at and why each piece feels different.
